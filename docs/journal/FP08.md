## What was learned

- **Convergence is real.** Each fix-pass has surfaced fewer findings on a smaller surface: FP05 (14+ findings), FP06 (4+3), FP07 (5+1), FP08 (1+1). The path-quoting contract has now been applied uniformly across `filter/`, `cli/`, `copy/errors`, `parser/errors`, and `copy/runner.py`'s warning emits. FP09 risk is low — at this point the surface should be path-quoting-clean.
- **`warnings.append(f"...")` grep is not exhaustive.** R1's lesson: Python supports multiple ways to populate a `list[str]` — `.append`, list comprehension, generator expression, `+=`, slice assignment. A grep on one form misses the others. The right audit pattern is to trace *value-flow into the destination type* (`CopyReport.warnings`) rather than searching for a single emit-shape. Logged in `docs/specs/FP08.md` § "Cluster R" for future fix-passes.
- **One-round spec-converge is achievable.** FP08's spec converged in a single cold-eyes round (READY TO SIGN OFF immediately). Reasons: (a) the fix-pass had a single bullet at the start; (b) the threat model and fix shape are identical to FP06/FP07 — no novel reasoning to verify; (c) the spec spent its words on the value-flow trace and the test scaffold reuse, both checkable in 5 minutes. When a fix-pass is genuinely small and shape-precedented, the cold-eyes round becomes a sanity check rather than a multi-round refinement.
- **Closing-review value scales with surface size, not finding count.** FP08's closing `/audit` + `/indie-review` (combined into one pass at this scale) found exactly one finding — but it was the right one. The audit-pattern lesson (R1's grep-vs-value-flow) is the kind of finding that compounds across future fix-passes; catching it once means future audits don't repeat the same scope error.
